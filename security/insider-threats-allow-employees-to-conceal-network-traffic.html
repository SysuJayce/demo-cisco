Insider Threats: Allow Employees to Conceal Network Traffic?

Martin Nystrom - December 10, 2014 - 2 Comments

You can lock every window and bolt every door to keep out intruders, but it wonâ€™t be of much use if the attacker is already inside; if the attacker is an insider. Most security reports and headlines highlight stories of organizations that are attacked by an external party, but incident statistics highlight a growing number of attacks from insiders and partners. These incidents are real, and threaten your most sensitive information. How do you know when an insider is exfiltrating data from your organization? In one particularly interesting incident, it was discovered an employee was encrypting and obfuscating outbound traffic from his laptop over a period of several weeks, using for-purchase VPN software called Internet Access" href="http://privateinternetaccess.com" target="_blank">Private Internet Access.

Detection

In this case, an analyst had discovered an internal employee using VPN within their corporate network. The VPN connections were made to Tor nodes identified using Cisco threat intelligence" href="http://tools.cisco.com/security/center/home.x" target="_blank">Cisco threat intelligence connected via Cisco FireSIGHT Management Center. This activity is often indicative of a person taking great pains to hide their network traffic, and can be done for criminal or privacy reasons.

Cisco Sourcefire Defense Center, showing connections to Tor exit nodes" width="550" height="239" />

Events displayed in Cisco FireSIGHT ManagementÂ Center, showing connections to Tor exit nodes

The analyst determined that the traffic was originating from a desktop network. The fact that it was routing through nodes visualized" href="http://hackertarget.com/tor-exit-node-visualization/" target="_blank">Tor exit nodes led the analyst to dig deeper. She quickly found some of the connections from this desktop were using self-signed certificates, which raised concern that malware such as TDSS or Dyreza were present on the userâ€™s device. She raised the case to a security investigator.

Certificate used by Dyreza

Investigation

The security investigator began investigating connection traffic for this desktop using full packet capture (FPC). The traffic evidenced use of Private Internet Access from the user device.

Full packet capture indicating connections to privateinternetaccess.com (host name has been obfuscated to prevent clicking on link.)" width="468" height="336" />

Upon reviewing the traffic and accompanying HTTP user agent, the investigator looked at NetFlow traffic to see how long this activity had been occurring. He discovered that the activity to Tor exit nodes using this VPN software had been underway for weeks. Given this activity and duration, he escalated a case to engage the employeeâ€™s manager and Human Resources (HR), advising them to interview the employee about the activity, including forensic examination of the desktop.

What Might this Mean?

There are several reasons why an employee or contractor might use these techniques to hide their traffic.

  1. Government restricts information to news and social media

Authoritarian countries often block traffic to news and social media sources. An employee working in that country may use products to route around these restrictions and hide their identity to avoid criminal consequences.

  1. Privacy zealot; highly suspicious of surveillance

Some employees, especially IT professionals, hold high standards for privacy, with corresponding suspicion of ISPs and employer monitoring. An employee may use these products to avoid detection.

  1. Access to deep web to buy or sell illegal products

Access to sites like Silk Road marketplace, which has since been shut down by law enforcement, or the Russian Anonymous MarketPlace (RAMP) can be accessed only via network traffic obfuscation such as Tor.

Some of the Russian-language banner ads advertising drugs for sale.

  1. Conducting activity that employer would consider suspicious or breach of employment contract

An employee with semi-advanced IT skill may use these tools to hide his traffic when accessing restricted sites, conducting job searches and interviews, or exfiltratingÂ sensitive information from his employer.

Remediation

The manager and HR interviewed the employee to discover why he was anonymizing and cloaking his traffic. At the end of the interview, the employee agreed to remove the software, and no further action was taken with the employee. Continuous monitoring confirmed that the traffic had ceased. However, this consequenceÂ was incongruous with the gravity of the employeeâ€™s activity. My recommendation for such cases is to do the following:

  1. Search logs and telemetry sourced from the employee

Determine if downloads from sensitive data stores were suspicious (much larger and/or more frequent than normal, accessing a broad range of data, or accessing documents well outside the scope of the employeeâ€™s assigned responsibilities.)

  1. Conduct a forensic examination of the employeeâ€™s devices

If warranted from step 1, extract a forensic image of the employeeâ€™s laptop and other work devices (smartphone, etc.), tying activity to timestamps extracted from step 1.

If employee malfeasance is discovered, assign consequences to the employee according to your data security policy and HR guidance.
